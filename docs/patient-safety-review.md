## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This review identifies **three critical findings requiring immediate action**, classifies all 20 patients into risk tiers, and provides 18 protocol safeguard and caregiver communication recommendations. The overall safety profile is characterized by a **100% IRR incidence (442 cumulative events), 65% SAE rate, 95% ADA positivity, and 85% Grade 3+ TEAE rate**. While no treatment discontinuations have occurred and no deaths have been reported, the cumulative AE burden in this pediatric population demands enhanced monitoring, particularly for the 4 patients classified as HIGH risk.

## 2. CRITICAL FINDINGS REQUIRING IMMEDIATE ACTION

### CRITICAL FINDING 1: Patient 0016-9006 Has Two Ongoing Unresolved SAEs Including Dysphagia

**Severity: URGENT**

Patient 0016-9006 (A3, 88-month-old female, Core Study) has two SAEs that are documented as "Not Resolved":
- **Mobility decreased** (Grade 2, Day 93, ongoing)
- **Dysphagia** (Grade 3, Day 119, ongoing)

Dysphagia in a pediatric MPS IIIA patient carries direct aspiration risk. While these may represent disease progression rather than treatment effects (both assessed as not drug-related), the following actions are required:

**Required Actions:**
1. Confirm that the site has implemented aspiration precautions and swallowing assessment
2. Ensure speech-language pathology and nutrition consultations are in place
3. Evaluate whether continued weekly IV infusions are safe given progressive functional decline
4. Document the clinical reasoning for continued treatment in the participant's file
5. Consider whether these events meet criteria for protocol-specified stopping rules

### CRITICAL FINDING 2: Anaphylaxis Preparedness for Cohort B1 Must Be Verified

**Severity: URGENT**

One anaphylactic reaction has occurred in the study (B1 cohort, 1/2 patients = 50% within-cohort incidence). Cohort B1 also shows:
- 100% moderate-to-severe IRR rate
- 100% dose interruption rate
- 100% ADA positivity with 100% neutralizing antibody confirmation
- The only drug-related SAE in the entire study (Grade 3 IRR, 0017-9004)
- SpO2 desaturation <92% (0017-9004, Day 388)

**Required Actions:**
1. Verify that all infusion sites (especially UNC Chapel Hill, the only B1 site) have updated anaphylaxis management kits, including weight-appropriate epinephrine auto-injectors at the infusion chair
2. Confirm post-infusion monitoring duration is a minimum of 2 hours for B1 patients (recommend extending to 3 hours given this cohort's profile)
3. Confirm nursing staff are PALS-certified and that a physician is immediately available during B1 infusions
4. Evaluate whether B1 patients should receive escalated pre-medication regimens compared to other cohorts

### CRITICAL FINDING 3: 95% ADA Positivity with 60% Neutralizing Antibodies Raises Long-Term Safety Concerns

**Severity: IMPORTANT -- Requires DEC Discussion**

- 19/20 patients (95%) are ADA positive at any post-baseline timepoint
- 12/20 (60%) have confirmed neutralizing ADA
- 3 patients identified with sustained high/very high titers: 0016-9004, 2065-9002, 0016-9003
- ADA positivity is associated with DEC-mandated dose adjustments in 7/20 (35%) participants
- B1 cohort: 100% neutralizing ADA

In a pediatric population expected to receive treatment for years (potentially decades), the cumulative immunogenicity burden is a first-order safety concern. Neutralizing antibodies may:
- Increase IRR frequency and severity over time
- Necessitate escalating pre-medication, with cumulative steroid exposure affecting growth
- Lead to infusion futility (loss of efficacy requiring higher doses, further increasing immunogenicity)

**Required Actions:**
1. DEC should review the ADA titer trajectory data and establish formal thresholds for dose modification or treatment pause
2. Develop a protocol amendment defining ADA-based treatment decision criteria
3. For the 3 highest-titer patients, request individual ADA trajectory plots and correlate with IRR frequency/severity over time
4. Assess whether an immune tolerance induction protocol should be considered

## 3. PEDIATRIC-SPECIFIC SAFETY CONCERNS

### 3.1 Impact of Weekly Infusions on Quality of Life

**Assessment: SIGNIFICANT CONCERN**

- Weekly IV infusions for children as young as 27 months represent an extraordinary caregiver and patient burden
- Cohort A1 patients have been on study >107 weeks (>2 years of weekly infusions)
- With 100% IRR incidence and 442 cumulative IRR events, **every infusion visit carries a near-certainty of symptomatic reaction** (vomiting 85%, pyrexia 80%, rash 60%)
- SAE of "inconsolable crying" (0017-9002, Grade 3) may reflect cumulative distress from repeated infusions
- 1 patient is documented as "active off study drug" -- the circumstances should be reviewed for potential treatment burden contribution

**Recommendations:**
1. Implement a formal **Caregiver Burden Assessment** (e.g., Zarit Burden Interview adapted for pediatric caregivers) at least quarterly
2. Provide caregivers with an IRR symptom diary to identify infusion-day patterns and recovery times
3. Consider whether infusion frequency can be reduced (e.g., biweekly) for participants who have achieved stable pharmacokinetic levels, particularly in the LTE phase
4. Ensure child life specialists or age-appropriate distraction/comfort measures are available at all infusion sites

### 3.2 Pre-Medication Adequacy for Pediatric Population

**Assessment: REQUIRES REVIEW**

The report documents pre-medications and dose modifications per DEC guidance, but the specific pre-medication regimen is not detailed. For a pediatric population with 100% IRR rates:

**Recommendations:**
1. Document the standard pre-medication regimen in each MMR (antihistamines, antipyretics, corticosteroids if used)
2. Assess cumulative corticosteroid exposure across all participants, especially regarding:
   - Linear growth velocity (growth charts should be reviewed at each visit)
   - Adrenal suppression risk with chronic steroid pre-medication
   - Behavioral effects (irritability is already reported in 40% of patients)
3. Consider whether ondansetron pre-medication should be standardized given 85% vomiting incidence
4. The weight trend data shows 19/20 patients with stable or increasing weight, but 1 patient has a weight plateau attributed to disease progression -- confirm this is not related to chronic vomiting/reduced oral intake from IRRs

### 3.3 Vital Sign Monitoring During Infusion

**Assessment: POTENTIALLY INSUFFICIENT**

Key findings from vital signs data:
- Peri-infusion tachycardia: 90% of patients (18/20)
- Temperature >38.5C peri-infusion: 80% (16/20)
- SpO2 desaturation <92%: 2 patients (10%) including 1 SAE
- QTcF >450ms (transient, IRR-related): 3 patients (15%)

**Recommendations:**
1. Vital signs should be monitored at minimum: pre-infusion, every 15 minutes during infusion, and every 30 minutes for at least 1 hour post-infusion (2 hours for B1 patients)
2. Continuous pulse oximetry should be mandated for all patients during infusion and for 1 hour post-infusion, given the 10% SpO2 desaturation rate
3. ECG monitoring should be available (not necessarily continuous) at all infusion sites given the QTcF findings and peri-infusion tachycardia in 90% of patients
4. Temperature monitoring should trigger a protocolized response algorithm (e.g., infusion rate reduction at >38C, infusion pause at >39C)

## 4. IRR MANAGEMENT ADEQUACY

### 4.1 Current IRR Profile Assessment

| Metric | Value | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| Any IRR | 20/20 (100%), 442 events | Universal; expected but requires management |
| Moderate-to-severe IRR | 15/20 (75%) | HIGH -- 3 in 4 patients experience significant reactions |
| Vomiting | 17/20 (85%), 84 events | HIGH -- most frequent symptom; pediatric concern |
| Pyrexia | 16/20 (80%), 79 events | HIGH -- febrile reactions in young children |
| Rash | 12/20 (60%), 108 events | MODERATE -- highest event count per patient |
| Anaphylaxis | 1/20 (5%), 1 event | CRITICAL -- life-threatening |
| Hypoxia | 3/20 (15%), 7 events | CONCERNING -- respiratory compromise |
| Tachycardia | 6/20 (30%), 10 events | CONCERNING -- cardiac stress |

### 4.2 IRR Protocol Sufficiency

**Assessment: CURRENT PROTOCOLS ARE INSUFFICIENT for the observed severity**

With 75% of patients experiencing moderate-to-severe IRRs and 442 cumulative events across 20 patients, the current pre-medication and dose modification approach has not adequately controlled IRR burden.

**Recommendations:**
1. **Infusion rate adjustment:** Implement a mandatory slow-start protocol if not already in place (e.g., 50% target rate for first 30 minutes, escalating in 25% increments every 30 minutes if tolerated)
2. **Graded response protocol:** Formalize a written IRR response algorithm:
   - Grade 1 (mild): Reduce infusion rate by 50%; monitor
   - Grade 2 (moderate): Pause infusion; administer rescue medications; resume at 50% rate only if resolved within 1 hour
   - Grade 3 (severe): Stop infusion; treat aggressively; do not resume same day; DEC notification required within 24 hours
   - Anaphylaxis: Full anaphylaxis protocol; DEC and sponsor emergency notification; mandatory infusion hold pending DEC review
3. **Pre-medication escalation:** For patients with recurrent moderate-severe IRRs, consider adding systemic corticosteroid pre-medication (if not already included) with appropriate pediatric growth monitoring safeguards
4. **Infusion environment:** Ensure PALS-certified personnel and resuscitation equipment are immediately available at all infusion sites

### 4.3 Anaphylaxis Management Plan

**Assessment: MUST BE VERIFIED AND ENHANCED**

Given 1 confirmed anaphylactic reaction:
1. All sites must have a documented, rehearsed anaphylaxis protocol specific to this study
2. Weight-based epinephrine dosing charts must be posted at each infusion station
3. Each infusion area must have: epinephrine (IM), diphenhydramine (IV), methylprednisolone (IV), albuterol (nebulized), oxygen, suction, and bag-valve-mask sized for pediatric patients
4. Post-infusion observation must be extended to minimum 2 hours for all patients (3 hours for B1 or any patient with prior anaphylaxis/severe IRR)
5. Annual anaphylaxis simulation drills should be mandated at each site

## 5. INFORMED CONSENT AND CAREGIVER COMMUNICATION

### 5.1 IRR Disclosure

**Assessment: MUST BE VERIFIED**

With a **100% IRR incidence**, informed consent documents must explicitly state:
- "All participants treated to date have experienced infusion-related reactions"
- The specific symptom profile: vomiting (85%), fever (80%), rash (60%)
- That 75% of participants have experienced moderate-to-severe reactions
- That 1 participant experienced an anaphylactic reaction
- That reactions can occur at any point during treatment, including after months of prior tolerance

**Recommendations:**
1. Review current ICF language to ensure the 100% IRR rate is explicitly stated
2. Provide caregivers with a written **"What to Expect on Infusion Day"** guide, including typical reaction patterns and recovery timeline
3. Ensure caregivers understand the distinction between expected IRRs and emergency symptoms requiring immediate intervention
4. Provide 24-hour contact information for study medical staff

### 5.2 Benefit-Risk Communication

**Assessment: REQUIRES CAREFUL CALIBRATION**

MPS IIIA is a fatal, progressive neurodegenerative disease with no approved treatments. This context significantly affects benefit-risk communication:

**Recommendations:**
1. Benefit-risk discussions must be documented at each consent update (initial, OLE, LTE)
2. Caregivers should be informed that:
   - 95% of patients develop anti-drug antibodies, which may affect long-term efficacy
   - The efficacy data (CSF heparan sulfate) is excluded from the safety report; caregivers should receive an appropriately blinded summary of whether there are signals of biological activity
   - The 65% SAE rate, while high, includes many events related to the underlying disease rather than the study drug
   - Only 1 SAE (5%) has been assessed as drug-related
3. Avoid therapeutic misconception: caregivers must understand this is a Phase 1/2 study primarily assessing safety, not a guaranteed treatment
4. A formal re-consent process should be triggered whenever a new safety signal emerges (e.g., post the anaphylaxis event, post any new drug-related SAE)

## 6. PROTOCOL SAFEGUARDS REVIEW

### 6.1 Stopping Rules Adequacy

**Assessment: POTENTIALLY INADEQUATE**

The 65% SAE rate is high, even accounting for the fact that most SAEs are assessed as unrelated to study drug. Key concerns:

| Factor | Finding | Concern Level |
|---|---|---|
| SAE rate | 13/20 (65%) | HIGH |
| Drug-related SAE | 1/20 (5%) | LOW |
| Grade 3+ TEAE rate | 17/20 (85%) | HIGH |
| Discontinuation for AE | 0/20 (0%) | LOW (possibly under-reported?) |
| Deaths | 0 | Reassuring |

**Recommendations:**
1. Current stopping rules should be reviewed to determine whether they include:
   - Cumulative toxicity thresholds (e.g., if >2 patients experience the same Grade 3+ drug-related SAE)
   - Individual patient stopping criteria based on cumulative AE burden (not just single events)
   - Specific criteria for IRR-based discontinuation (e.g., >3 Grade 3 IRRs in a single patient)
2. The zero discontinuation rate in the context of 85% Grade 3+ TEAEs and 100% IRR suggests either (a) highly effective management or (b) a potential reluctance to discontinue in a fatal disease with no alternatives -- a bias that should be acknowledged and monitored
3. Stopping rules should include ADA-based criteria (e.g., sustained high-titer neutralizing ADA with concurrent loss of biomarker response)

### 6.2 DEC/DSMB Oversight Frequency

**Assessment: SHOULD BE INCREASED FOR B1 COHORT**

**Recommendations:**
1. DEC review frequency for B1 cohort should be increased to monthly (given 100% SAE rate, anaphylaxis event, and only drug-related SAE in the study)
2. The DEC should receive individual patient safety narratives for all HIGH-risk tier patients at each review
3. An independent DSMB (if not already in place) should be convened at least semi-annually for a holistic safety review separate from the DEC's dose-escalation mandate
4. The DEC should formally review the 1 patient "active off study drug" to understand whether this represents a safety signal

### 6.3 Site-to-Site Safety Variation Monitoring

**Assessment: POTENTIAL CONCERN**

The study is conducted at 4 US sites with unequal enrollment:
- UNC Chapel Hill (0017): 8 patients -- highest enrollment, hosts all B1 patients
- UCSF Benioff (0016): 5 patients
- University of Iowa (2064): 4 patients
- Baylor/TCH (2065): 3 patients

**Recommendations:**
1. Compare IRR incidence rates per infusion across sites (not just per participant) to identify whether any site has systematically higher or lower IRR rates, which may reflect differences in infusion practice, pre-medication adherence, or reporting thresholds
2. The concentration of all B1 (highest risk) patients at a single site (UNC) means site-level risk is not evenly distributed -- ensure UNC has adequate resources and oversight
3. SAE reporting timeliness should be audited across sites, particularly given the post-publication data updates (e.g., 0017-9007 dyskinesia SAE confirmed only in post-publication update)
4. Protocol deviation rates by site should be tracked (UNC has 3 of 4 documented deviations -- sample size may explain this, but it should be monitored)

## 7. LONG-TERM SAFETY CONSIDERATIONS

### 7.1 Immunogenicity Trajectory

**Assessment: FIRST-ORDER LONG-TERM SAFETY CONCERN**

- 95% ADA positivity and 60% neutralizing ADA at this stage of the study sets a trajectory of concern
- Cohort A1 patients (longest exposure, >107 weeks) have 100% ADA positivity and 75% neutralizing ADA
- Cohort B1 (100% neutralizing ADA) shows the most concerning immunogenicity-safety correlation
- The 3 patients with sustained high titers (0016-9004, 2065-9002, 0016-9003) are sentinel cases for long-term immunogenicity outcomes

**Recommendations:**
1. Establish a formal **Immunogenicity Monitoring Plan** with defined titer thresholds triggering:
   - Increased IRR monitoring frequency
   - Pre-medication escalation
   - Dose modification or treatment pause
   - Consideration of immune tolerance protocols
2. Correlate ADA titer trajectories with:
   - IRR frequency and severity over time per patient
   - Biomarker response (CSF HS, urine HS) to assess efficacy impact
   - Need for DEC-mandated dose adjustments
3. For patients entering multi-year extension phases, establish annual immunogenicity deep-dives including neutralizing antibody characterization

### 7.2 Growth and Development Monitoring

**Assessment: ADEQUATE BUT SHOULD BE ENHANCED**

- Weight trends are stable or increasing in 19/20 patients (reassuring)
- 1 patient has a weight plateau attributed to disease progression
- Height/linear growth data is not reported separately -- this is a gap for a pediatric population receiving potential chronic corticosteroid pre-medication

**Recommendations:**
1. Report height, weight, and BMI z-scores (age-adjusted) in each MMR
2. Track growth velocity against MPS IIIA-specific growth curves (if available) or standard pediatric growth charts
3. For patients receiving corticosteroid pre-medication, monitor for growth deceleration, glucose intolerance, and adrenal insufficiency
4. Neurodevelopmental assessments (KABC, VABS-III, BSID) are collected per protocol (Section 3.3/Appendix A1) -- while results are excluded from this safety report, the medical monitor should ensure these are reviewed for safety signals (developmental regression beyond expected disease trajectory)

### 7.3 Cumulative Organ Toxicity Monitoring

- Liver: No Hy's Law cases; ALT, AST, bilirubin within limits -- adequate monitoring
- Renal: Creatinine within normal limits -- adequate
- Cardiac: Transient QTcF prolongation in 3 patients, SVT SAE in 1 -- warrants continued ECG monitoring
- Hematologic: Eosinophil elevations peri-IRR (consistent), mild lymphocyte reductions in A1 -- lymphocyte subset monitoring should be considered for long-term patients
- CK: Transient elevation in A3 -- monitor for myopathy signals

## 9. RISK-BENEFIT ASSESSMENT STATEMENT

Despite the significant and universal AE burden (100% IRR, 65% SAE, 85% Grade 3+), the benefit-risk assessment for this study remains **conditionally favorable**, given:

**Favorable factors:**
- MPS IIIA is a uniformly fatal neurodegenerative disease with no approved therapies
- Zero treatment discontinuations and zero deaths
- Only 1/20 participants (5%) had a drug-related SAE
- Most SAEs are attributable to underlying disease or intercurrent illness
- Liver, renal, and cardiac safety profiles remain acceptable
- All 20 participants (or their caregivers) have consented to extension phases

**Concerning factors:**
- 100% IRR incidence with 442 cumulative events creates substantial ongoing patient/caregiver burden
- 95% ADA positivity with 60% neutralizing antibodies threatens long-term efficacy
- 1 anaphylactic reaction in a 20-patient study (5%) is noteworthy
- Patient 0016-9006 has progressive functional decline (ongoing dysphagia and mobility loss)
- The long-term immunogenicity trajectory is uncertain

**Conditional on:**
- Implementation of the critical and short-term actions listed above
- Adequate DEC/DSMB oversight of the HIGH-risk patients
- Evidence of biological activity (CSF HS reduction) justifying continued exposure
- Regular re-assessment of individual patient benefit-risk at each extension consent
