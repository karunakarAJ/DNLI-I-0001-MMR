## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

DNL-126 (ETV:SGSH-BioM) demonstrates a safety profile dominated by infusion-related reactions (IRRs) in a vulnerable pediatric population with a uniformly fatal underlying disease. Through the 2026-03-20 data cut, 20/20 participants have been treated, with 19 remaining active on study drug and zero discontinuations due to adverse events.

**Overall Risk Assessment: FAVORABLE, with caveats requiring active surveillance.**

The safety data reveal three principal areas of concern requiring ongoing vigilance:

1. **IRR burden remains the dominant safety signal.** 442 cumulative IRR events with 100% incidence and 75% moderate-to-severe rate indicate that current pre-medication strategies are partially effective but insufficient to prevent clinically significant reactions. One anaphylactic reaction (0017-9004, Cohort B1) and 7 hypoxia events (3 participants) represent the most serious manifestations. Temporal data suggest IRR frequency attenuates with prolonged exposure (A1 rate approximately 0.28/patient-week vs. A3 approximately 0.66/patient-week), which is reassuring.

2. **Immunogenicity is near-universal.** 95% ADA positivity and 60% neutralizing antibody prevalence correlate with the IRR burden and have driven DEC-mandated dose adjustments in at least 7 participants (35%). Cohort B1, with 100% neutralizing antibody positivity, carries the highest per-patient IRR event rate and the only anaphylactic reaction, raising the question of whether younger age and/or specific immunogenic profiles predispose to more severe reactions.

3. **Participant 0017-9004 (Cohort B1) represents a sentinel case.** This 27-month-old male has accumulated 4 SAEs (seizure-like activity, pneumonia, Grade 3 drug-related IRR, and hypoxia), plus the single anaphylactic reaction event. The classification of the Day 388 hypoxia SAE as "Not Related" warrants re-adjudication given the temporal and mechanistic plausibility of IRR/anaphylaxis-mediated respiratory compromise.

**No deaths, no Hy's Law cases, no QTcF >480ms, no drug-related discontinuations, and no late-onset organ toxicity signals** have been identified through up to 107 weeks of exposure. The benefit-risk balance for an untreatable fatal neurodegenerative disease remains favorable.

**Clinical interpretation:** The 100% TEAE rate and 85% Grade >=3 rate are expected and consistent with the class effect of IV enzyme replacement therapies in lysosomal storage disorders, compounded by the natural history of MPS IIIA in young children. #### 1.2 SAE Analysis -- 15 Events in 13 Participants

**SAE clustering by participant:**
- **0017-9004 (B1, 27M):** 4 SAEs -- highest individual burden. Seizure-like activity (Day 42), pneumonia (Day 48), drug-related Grade 3 IRR (Day 283), hypoxia (Day 388). This pattern is clinically concerning: two SAEs within 6 days (Days 42-48) suggest acute vulnerability, and two respiratory/IRR-related SAEs later in treatment (Days 283, 388) suggest persistent hypersensitivity risk.
- **0016-9006 (A3, 88F):** 3 SAEs including 2 unresolved (mobility decreased Day 93, dysphagia Day 119). These likely reflect MPS IIIA disease progression given this participant's older age (88 months = 7.3 years), but the temporal proximity to treatment initiation warrants confirmation.
- **0017-9007 (A3, 44M):** 2 SAEs including MRSA bacteremia (Day 2, pre-treatment) and dyskinesia (Day 163, post-publication update). The dyskinesia SAE is atypical for MPS IIIA natural history and requires further characterization.

**SAE clustering by body system:**
- Infections: 3 SAEs (Staph bacteraemia, pneumonia, MRSA) -- expected in pediatric patients with chronic IV access devices
- Nervous system: 3 SAEs (seizure-like activity, cognitive disorder, dyskinesia) -- first two consistent with MPS IIIA natural history; dyskinesia is atypical
- GI/MSK disease progression: 2 SAEs (dysphagia, mobility decreased) in 0016-9006 -- likely disease-related
- IRR: 1 SAE (drug-related, 0017-9004) -- expected class effect
- Respiratory: 1 SAE (hypoxia, 0017-9004) -- causality questionable (see below)
- Cardiac: 1 SAE (SVT, 0016-9001, Day 16) -- early, isolated, resolved
- Other: 3 SAEs (post-LP headache, inconsolable crying, delirium, port infiltration) -- procedural/disease-related

**SAE clustering by site:**
- Site 0017 (UNC): 8/15 SAEs (53%), but also enrolls 8/20 participants (40%). Adjusted SAE rate is modestly higher (1.0 SAE/participant vs. 0.75 overall). Not statistically meaningful in this small sample but worth monitoring.

**SAE clustering by timeline:**
- 3 SAEs occurred pre-treatment (Days -20, -20, -11) -- attributable to procedures or pre-existing conditions
- No temporal clustering pattern among the 12 on-treatment SAEs (Days 16-638)
- No evidence of late-onset cumulative toxicity in the longest-exposed cohorts (A1: up to Day 638)

**Causality concern -- 0017-9004 hypoxia SAE:** The Day 388 hypoxia SAE is classified "Not Related" despite:
- Same participant had the only drug-related SAE (Grade 3 IRR, Day 283)
- Same participant had the only anaphylactic reaction event
- Hypoxia is a recognized manifestation of severe IRR/anaphylaxis
- SpO2 desaturation <92% was recorded in this participant (Section 8)

This causality assessment should be formally re-adjudicated. Even if the hypoxia event occurred outside the immediate peri-infusion window, a delayed or biphasic hypersensitivity mechanism cannot be excluded without a detailed case narrative.

**Signal Assessment: MEDIUM risk. No unexpected safety signals. 0017-9004 burden and 0017-9007 dyskinesia require focused follow-up.**

### 2. IRR Profile (Section 5.2)

#### 2.1 IRR Burden Summary

442 cumulative IRR events across 20 participants with the following symptom hierarchy:

| Symptom | Incidence n/N (%) | Total Events | Events per Affected Patient |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rash | 12/20 (60%) | 108 | 9.0 |
| Vomiting | 17/20 (85%) | 84 | 4.9 |
| Pyrexia | 16/20 (80%) | 79 | 4.9 |
| Irritability | 8/20 (40%) | 40 | 5.0 |
| Urticaria | 7/20 (35%) | 16 | 2.3 |
| Nausea | 7/20 (35%) | 13 | 1.9 |
| Tachycardia | 6/20 (30%) | 10 | 1.7 |
| Erythema | 7/20 (35%) | 10 | 1.4 |
| Cough | 6/20 (30%) | 9 | 1.5 |
| Hypoxia | 3/20 (15%) | 7 | 2.3 |
| Flushing | 7/20 (35%) | 8 | 1.1 |
| Chills | 5/20 (25%) | 5 | 1.0 |
| Anaphylactic reaction | 1/20 (5%) | 1 | 1.0 |

**Key observations:**

1. **Rash is the most recurrent symptom** (108 events, 9.0 per affected patient). This is notable for A2 (38 events in 3 participants = 12.7/pt) and A3 (61 events in 7 participants = 8.7/pt), suggesting immune-mediated cutaneous reactivity that persists with ongoing dosing.

2. **Temporal attenuation pattern:** The data strongly suggest that IRR frequency decreases with prolonged exposure:
   - Cohort A1 (median 107 weeks): 118 events / 4 pts = 29.5 events/pt, ~0.28/pt-week
   - Cohort A2 (median 91 weeks): 91 events / 4 pts = 22.8 events/pt, ~0.25/pt-week
   - Cohort A3 (median 26 weeks): 172 events / 10 pts = 17.2 events/pt, ~0.66/pt-week
   - Cohort B1 (median 56 weeks): 61 events / 2 pts = 30.5 events/pt, ~0.54/pt-week

   A1 and A2 show substantially lower per-week rates than A3, consistent with immune tolerance development over time.

3. **B1 has the highest per-patient event rate (30.5)** despite only 56 weeks median exposure, translating to 0.54 events/pt-week -- nearly double the A1 rate. With only 2 participants, this could reflect participant-level variability, but the youngest cohort (27 months) having the highest rate warrants attention.

4. **Hypoxia events cluster in A3 and B1** (6 events in 2 A3 participants, 1 event in B1). This represents the most clinically concerning IRR manifestation below the threshold of anaphylaxis and suggests that respiratory monitoring should be prioritized in newer cohorts.

#### 2.2 Anaphylactic Reaction (B1, 0017-9004)

This single event requires careful characterization:
- **Cohort B1** contains the youngest participants (27 months at baseline)
- **0017-9004** has 100% neutralizing ADA, 4 SAEs, and the only drug-related SAE
- **No anaphylactic events** have occurred in A1, A2, or A3 cohorts
- The event resolved, and the participant remains on study drug (OLE)

**Questions for the medical monitor:**
- Was epinephrine administered? Was the Brighton Collaboration criteria assessment completed?
- What was the temporal relationship to infusion start and dose level at the time?
- Were pre-medications modified after this event?
- Has the participant had subsequent infusions without recurrence?

#### 2.3 Pre-medication Adequacy Assessment

75% of participants (15/20) experienced moderate-to-severe IRR despite prophylactic pre-medications. This indicates that:
- Current pre-medication regimen provides partial but incomplete protection
- The high rate of pyrexia (80%), vomiting (85%), and rash (60%) suggests both cytokine release and IgE-mediated components
- DEC-mandated dose modifications in 7 participants (35%) represent a necessary but reactive approach
- Proactive escalation of pre-medication (e.g., addition of montelukast, higher-dose corticosteroids, or anti-IgE therapy for recurrent urticaria/anaphylaxis) should be considered

**Signal Assessment: MEDIUM-HIGH risk. IRR is the dominant safety liability. Temporal attenuation is reassuring. The anaphylactic reaction and hypoxia events represent the upper end of the severity spectrum requiring robust emergency preparedness.**

### 3. Hepatotoxicity / eDISH (Section 6.4)

**Clean eDISH finding validated:**
- ALT >3xULN: 0/20
- AST >3xULN: 0/20
- Total Bilirubin >1.5xULN: 0/20
- Total Bilirubin >2xULN: 0/20
- No participants in Hy's Law quadrant (ALT >3xULN AND TBili >2xULN)
- Creatinine >1.5xULN: 0/20

**CK elevation in Cohort A3:** A transient CK elevation >5xULN was noted but resolved. In the context of MPS IIIA -- a disease characterized by progressive skeletal and muscular involvement -- CK elevations may reflect disease activity (muscle turnover, physical therapy, or seizure-related rhabdomyolysis) rather than drug toxicity. However, CK should be tracked longitudinally, particularly in participants approaching the 10 mg/kg dose level, to exclude drug-related myotoxicity.

**Monitoring frequency adequacy:** Safety labs are transferred monthly from MLM. For hepatic parameters, this frequency is adequate given:
- Zero exceedances through 107 weeks of maximum exposure
- No historical signal for hepatotoxicity with SGSH enzyme replacement
- Continued monthly lab draws per protocol Schedule of Assessments

**Recommendation:** No change to monitoring frequency. Continue eDISH analysis at each data cut. Request individual ALT/AST/TBili longitudinal scatter plots to detect sub-threshold trending that might not be captured by categorical thresholds alone.

**Signal Assessment: LOW risk.**

### 4. ECG Safety (Section 7)

| Parameter | A1 (N=4) | A2 (N=4) | A3 (N=10) | B1 (N=2) | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| QTcF >450ms (any visit) | 1 (25%) | 0 | 1 (10%) | 1 (50%) | Single observations, non-persistent |
| QTcF >480ms | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | None |
| QTcF >500ms | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | None |
| PR >200ms | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | None |
| HR >100bpm peri-infusion | 3 (75%) | 2 (50%) | 4 (40%) | 2 (100%) | Expected with IRR |

**Clinical interpretation of QTcF >450ms in 3/20:**

The 15% incidence of QTcF >450ms is not clinically alarming for the following reasons:
1. All were **single observations** (not persistent or progressive)
2. The Fridericia correction is sensitive to heart rate, and these events correlated with **peri-infusion tachycardia** (HR >100bpm in 55% of participants)
3. **No QTcF >480ms or >500ms** events have been observed
4. **No PR prolongation or QRS widening** suggesting conduction system toxicity
5. The pediatric MPS IIIA population may have baseline cardiac variability (note 0016-9001 had an SVT SAE at Day 16)

**However,** the 50% rate in B1 (1/2 participants) should be monitored. With the youngest patients and highest IRR severity, the combined hemodynamic stress (tachycardia + temperature elevation + potential cytokine effects) could predispose to transient QT prolongation.

**Recommendation:** Consider obtaining ECGs both pre-infusion and at a standardized time point during/after infusion to separate baseline QTcF from IRR-related changes. If any participant develops QTcF >480ms, cardiology consultation should be obtained.

**Signal Assessment: LOW risk.**

| Parameter | Incidence | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| SpO2 <92% | 2/20 (10%) | A3: 1/10, B1: 1/2 -- see below |
| Temp >38.5C peri-infusion | 16/20 (80%) | Expected with IRR; manageable |

| SBP Grade >=2 elevation | 1/20 (5%) | Transient, peri-IRR, resolved |
| Weight stable/increasing | 19/20 (95%) | Expected pediatric growth |

#### 5.1 SpO2 Desaturation Analysis

Two participants experienced SpO2 <92%:
- **0017-9004 (B1):** Hypoxia SAE at Day 388, managed clinically. This participant has the most complex safety profile in the study (4 SAEs, anaphylactic reaction, drug-related IRR).
- **1 participant in A3:** Details limited in the report. Given that A3 also has 6 hypoxia IRR events across 2 participants, this likely represents the same individual(s).

**Pattern assessment:** SpO2 desaturation is NOT isolated -- it correlates with the hypoxia IRR events (7 total across 3 participants) and clusters in the newer/younger cohorts (A3, B1). This suggests a relationship with immune status (higher ADA titers early in treatment) and/or age-related airway vulnerability.

#### 5.2 Temperature Elevation

80% of participants (16/20) experienced temperature >38.5C during infusion. This is consistent with:
- Cytokine release during enzyme infusion
- IRR-mediated pyrexia (79 pyrexia IRR events)
- The 20% without fever (4 participants) may have more effective pre-medication coverage or lower immunoreactivity

Current management with antipyretics as part of pre-medication appears adequate, as febrile episodes are transient and resolve. No febrile neutropenia or infectious complications have been attributed to peri-infusion fever.

**Signal Assessment: LOW-MEDIUM risk. SpO2 desaturation pattern warrants focused respiratory monitoring in A3 and B1.**

### 6. Lab Safety Trending (Section 6)

#### 6.1 Biochemistry

No CTCAE Grade >=2 exceedances for ALT, AST, total bilirubin, or creatinine. The data cut added 518 new lab records with no new threshold exceedances.

#### 6.2 CK Elevation in Cohort A3

**Significance assessment:** MPS IIIA involves progressive musculoskeletal deterioration. CK elevations in this context may reflect:
- Disease-related muscle breakdown
- Seizure-related rhabdomyolysis (seizure-like activity is a known disease feature)
- Post-procedural effects (LP, port access)
- Drug-related myotoxicity (less likely given transience and single-cohort occurrence)

**Recommendation:** Track CK values longitudinally across all cohorts. If CK elevations recur or increase in magnitude with dose escalation to 10 mg/kg, consider targeted myotoxicity evaluation (aldolase, myoglobin, muscle imaging).

#### 6.3 Hematology

- No Grade >=2 anemia
- No clinically significant thrombocytopenia
- Transient eosinophilia peri-IRR in A3 and B1: consistent with allergic/hypersensitivity component of IRR; supports consideration of anti-allergic pre-medication enhancement
- Mild lymphocyte reductions in A1: monitored, no clinical significance. Worth continued surveillance given the immunomodulatory nature of enzyme replacement and chronic ADA formation

**Signal Assessment: LOW risk. CK trending and eosinophilia patterns warrant longitudinal tracking.**

### 7. Immunogenicity-Safety Correlation (Sections 4.2, 5.2)

| ADA Parameter | A1 (N=4) | A2 (N=4) | A3 (N=10) | B1 (N=2) | All (N=20) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| ADA positive (any timepoint) | 4 (100%) | 4 (100%) | 9 (90%) | 2 (100%) | 19 (95%) |
| Neutralizing ADA | 3 (75%) | 2 (50%) | 5 (50%) | 2 (100%) | 12 (60%) |
| DEC dose adjustments (ADA-associated) | 2 (50%) | 2 (50%) | 3 (30%) | 0* | 7 (35%) |

*B1 participants have not required DEC-mandated dose adjustments despite 100% neutralizing ADA, which is notable.

**Key correlations:**

1. **ADA positivity and IRR:** The 1 ADA-negative participant (A3) provides a natural control. Comparison of this participant's IRR profile vs. ADA-positive participants would be informative.

2. **Neutralizing ADA and IRR severity:** B1 (100% neutralizing) has the highest per-patient IRR event rate (30.5), the only anaphylactic reaction, and the only drug-related SAE. A1 (75% neutralizing) has 29.5 events/patient but over twice the exposure duration, indicating a lower event rate. A2 (50% neutralizing) has the lowest per-patient event count (22.8). This gradient is suggestive but not definitive with N=20.

3. **Baseline ADA positivity:** 5/20 (25%) were ADA-positive at baseline (1 A2, 4 A3). These participants may have pre-existing cross-reactive antibodies. Whether baseline ADA-positive status correlates with earlier or more severe IRR onset is unknown and should be analyzed.

4. **Specific high-titer participants:** The report identifies 0016-9004 (neutralizing, persistent), 2065-9002 (very high titer), and 0016-9003 (persistent positive) as key ADA-positive participants. Individual-level ADA titer trajectories correlated with IRR event timelines would be the most informative analysis.

**Signal Assessment: MEDIUM risk. Near-universal immunogenicity is expected for a non-native protein therapeutic but the 60% neutralizing rate and correlation with clinical outcomes (IRR, dose modifications) requires formal quantitative analysis.**

### 8. Dose-Response Safety (Sections 4.1, 5)

**Dose escalation scheme across cohorts:**
- All cohorts start at 3 mg/kg QW and escalate to 10 mg/kg (target dose)
- 17/20 (85%) have reached 10 mg/kg
- 3 A3 participants remain at lower dose levels (likely still in escalation phase given median 26 weeks on study)
- 12/20 (60%) have had at least 1 dose interruption
- 7/20 (35%) have had DEC-mandated dose adjustments

**Dose-response observations:**

1. **No clear dose-dependent toxicity escalation:** A1 participants have been at 10 mg/kg for the longest duration (approaching 2 years) without late-onset safety signals. This is reassuring for long-term tolerability at the target dose.

2. **IRR rates are highest during early treatment (dose escalation phase):** A3 has the highest per-week IRR rate despite the majority still at lower dose levels, suggesting that IRR correlates more with immune sensitization timing than absolute dose level.

3. **B1 (youngest cohort, both at 10 mg/kg) has the most concerning safety profile:** However, this likely reflects age-related vulnerability (immature immune regulation, smaller airway caliber increasing hypoxia risk) rather than dose-response per se.

4. **Compliance remains high:** >=91% across all cohorts despite dose interruptions, indicating that the dose modification protocols successfully maintain participants on therapy.

**Signal Assessment: LOW-MEDIUM risk. No dose-dependent worsening. Age may be a more important predictor of safety profile than dose level.**

### Benefit-Risk Conclusion

**FAVORABLE.** MPS IIIA (Sanfilippo Syndrome Type A) is a fatal, progressive neurodegenerative disorder with no approved disease-modifying therapy. Affected children experience relentless cognitive decline, behavioral deterioration, and ultimately death, typically by the second or third decade of life. In this context:

- **No drug-related deaths or permanent injuries** have occurred
- **Zero discontinuations** for adverse events across 20 participants with up to 107 weeks of exposure
- The **dominant safety liability (IRR)** is a known class effect of IV enzyme replacement therapy, shows temporal attenuation, and is actively managed through pre-medication and dose modification
- **Hepatic, renal, cardiac, and hematologic safety** profiles are clean
- The **immunogenicity profile** is consistent with expectations for a non-native protein therapeutic and does not preclude continued treatment

The benefit-risk balance supports continued dosing with enhanced monitoring for the identified signals detailed below.

## ACTION ITEMS / RECOMMENDATIONS FOR THE MEDICAL MONITOR

### Priority 1 -- Immediate (Before Next DEC/DSMB Meeting)

1. **Re-adjudicate 0017-9004 hypoxia SAE causality.** Request a comprehensive case narrative covering the Day 283 drug-related IRR SAE, the anaphylactic reaction event, and the Day 388 hypoxia SAE. Present the temporal sequence and mechanistic plausibility of delayed hypersensitivity to the DSMB for independent causality assessment.

2. **Characterize the anaphylactic reaction in 0017-9004.** Confirm whether Brighton Collaboration criteria were applied, whether epinephrine was administered, the temporal relationship to infusion and dose level, and whether subsequent infusions were tolerated. Update the Investigator's Brochure anaphylaxis section if needed.

3. **Evaluate 0017-9007 dyskinesia SAE.** Dyskinesia is atypical for MPS IIIA natural history. Request neurology consultation notes, concomitant medication review (rule out drug-drug interaction), and determine whether this represents a novel neurological signal vs. atypical disease manifestation. This was a post-publication update and may not have been reviewed by the DSMB.

### Priority 2 -- Next Monthly Review Cycle

4. **Commission formal ADA-IRR correlation analysis.** Request the biostatistics team to analyze individual-level ADA titer trajectories against IRR event frequency, severity, and symptom type. Include baseline ADA-positive participants as a subgroup.

5. **Review pre-medication protocol adequacy with the DEC.** 75% moderate-to-severe IRR rate despite prophylaxis suggests the current regimen is insufficient. Consider:
   - Addition of montelukast or other leukotriene receptor antagonist for recurrent urticaria/rash
   - Higher-dose or longer-duration corticosteroid pre-treatment for participants with recurrent pyrexia/vomiting
   - Anti-IgE therapy (omalizumab) consideration for the participant with anaphylactic reaction history
   - Standardized escalation pathway tied to ADA titer thresholds

6. **Confirm disease progression vs. drug effect in 0016-9006.** Two unresolved SAEs (mobility decreased Day 93, dysphagia Day 119) in a 7.3-year-old are consistent with MPS IIIA progression but occurred relatively early after treatment initiation. Request investigator assessment of whether these represent expected disease trajectory or potential acceleration.

### Priority 3 -- Ongoing Surveillance

7. **Implement longitudinal CK tracking.** Add CK trend analysis to each MMR cycle. Track across all cohorts, not just A3, to detect any dose- or exposure-related pattern.

8. **Request individual-level sub-threshold liver function plots.** While eDISH is clean, individual ALT/AST/TBili scatter plots over time would detect emerging trends before categorical thresholds are crossed.

9. **Standardize peri-infusion ECG timing.** Obtain ECGs both pre-infusion and at a standardized time point (e.g., 2 hours post-infusion start) to separate baseline QTcF from IRR-related changes. This will clarify whether QTcF >450ms events are truly transient.

10. **Monitor for eosinophilia trends.** Transient peri-IRR eosinophilia in A3 and B1 is consistent with allergic/hypersensitivity mechanisms. If eosinophilia becomes persistent or increases in magnitude, this would support an IgE-mediated component and guide pre-medication modification.

11. **Track the 1 ADA-negative participant (A3).** This participant serves as a natural comparator. Document whether their IRR frequency and severity differ from ADA-positive participants.
